Abstract 

Post-training large language models with rein-forcement learning is bottlenecked by the re-ward signal. Existing approaches require ei-ther ground-truth verifiable rewards, restrict-ing training to domains with automatic cor-rectness checks (e.g., mathematics, code ex-ecution), or human preference labels, which are expensive to collect and prone to reward hacking. Recent label-free methods replace ground-truth verifiers with self-referential sig-nals like majority voting or token entropy over a model’s own outputs, but risk reinforcing a model’s own errors. In this work we pro-pose Cross-Model Entropy (CME), the mean log-likelihood of a generator’s response un-der a separate verifier model, as a label-free reward signal for RL post-training. CME is continuous, training-free, and grounded in the principle that responses a verifier finds unsur-prising are likely correct or high quality. Be-cause the verifier is independent of the gener-ator, the signal cannot be gamed through self-consistency. We integrate CME into GRPO with no other changes to the training loop, ex-tending label-free RL to open-ended instruction following—a regime where self-referential sig-nals are inapplicable or poorly suited. On open-ended instruction following (UltraFeedback prompts, evaluated on AlpacaEval 2.0), CME rewards beat the untrained base in head-to-head LLM-as-Judge comparisons across four model families (Qwen, Llama, Gemma, OLMo) and three training regimes (pretrained, SFT, and instruction-tuned), with tie-adjusted win rates ranging from 52 .5% to 71 .4% . Code will be released upon publication.

These approaches rely on ground-truth verifiers, which restrict training to domains where correctness is automatically check-able. The alternative is reward models trained from human preference data (Ouyang et al., 2022), with variants like DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023) eliminat-ing the reward model and RLAIF (Bai et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2023) substituting an LLM judge for human annotators. TTRL (Zuo et al., 2025) and Evol-RL (Zhou et al., 2025) use majority voting over sampled responses as a pseudo-label for GRPO, with Evol-RL adding a novelty term to mitigate the “cognitive collapse” that majority-voting alone can induce. RENT (Prabhudesai et al., 2025), EM-RL (Agarwal et al., 2025), and IN-TUITOR (Zhao et al., 2026) take a different route, using the generator’s own token entropy or self-certainty as an intrinsic reward that pushes the pol-icy toward sharper output distributions. While each of these methods is effective in its target setting — typically math or code with verifiable structure — they share a common property: the reward derives entirely from the generator’s own outputs. We refer to such rewards as self-referential . The risk is struc-tural: when the model is systematically wrong in a way multiple rollouts agree on, or assigns high con-fidence to incorrect outputs, the reward reinforces the error rather than correcting it. Majority-voting variants additionally require a canonical answer per prompt and do not extend to open-ended generation — ruling out the quality and alignment objectives that motivate modern post-training (Rafailov et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2022). We propose a different approach. Instead of asking the generator to vote on its own outputs or measure its own confidence, we ask whether a separate capable model finds the generator’s re-sponse surprising. Cross-Model Entropy (CME), the negative log-likelihood of a generator’s re-sponse under a separate verifier model, is a con-tinuous, token-level, training-free signal that re-quires no ground-truth labels. Low CME indicates the verifier finds the response unsurprising. Maxi-mizing expected CME minimizes the reverse KL 

DKL (πθ ∥ πϕ) up to a generator-entropy term, a useful target when the verifier is more competent in-domain than the generator. The approach is vi-able because open-weight instruction-tuned models like Gemma-3 (Gemma Team, 2025) are capable enough to serve as meaningful evaluators of re-sponse quality, even when they are not frontier models. We integrate CME into GRPO with no other changes to the training loop, extending label-free RL to open-ended instruction following—the regime that motivates modern post-training and where self-referential signals are inapplicable or poorly suited. We train on a subset of Ul-traFeedback and evaluate AlpacaEval 2.0 win rate (Dubois et al., 2024) using LLM-as-Judge, finding that CME rewards beat the untrained base across four model families (Qwen, Llama, Gemma, OLMo) and three training regimes (pretrained, SFT, instruction-tuned). On SFT backbones, CME-GRPO matches DPO trained on the same base us-ing only UltraFeedback prompts and none of its preference annotations. We additionally show that performance scales with verifier capability, and that a randomly initialized verifier control under-performs every real-weighted verifier—confirming that the signal comes from cross-model evaluation rather than generic regularization.

3 Experiments 

We fine-tune generators spanning four families (Qwen, Llama, Gemma, OLMo) and three training regimes (pretrained, SFT, and instruction-tuned), in each case using gemma-3n-E4B-it (Gemma Team, 2025) as a frozen verifier. Training uses a subset of UltraFeedback (Cui et al., 2024) with all preference annotations and chosen/rejected completions dis-carded; only the prompts are retained. The reward is the token-level CME from Eq. (1) computed over the full response, with character-level alignment to handle the cross-family tokenizer mismatch. This setup stresses the cross-family setting: the verifier shares no family with most generators, and for pre-trained backbones it must provide useful gradient signal to models that have never been instruction-tuned. Full training details are in Appendix E. 

Evaluation. We evaluate on the 805-prompt Al-pacaEval 2.0 set (Dubois et al., 2024), replacing the default GPT-4 reference with gemma-3-1b-it 

— a 1B instruction-tuned model comparable in size to the models under test. For each experi-ment we draw greedy responses from up to four models: the base checkpoint, our CME-GRPO fine-tune, the same-family instruct checkpoint, and the 

gemma-3-1b-it reference. We run pairwise GPT-5.2 and Claude Sonnet 4.6 LLM-as-Judge (Zheng et al., 2023) evaluations per prompt with random-ized presentation order, and report tie-adjusted win rate (wins + 12 ties )/N over N = 805 prompts. Direct head-to-head comparisons (Base vs. CME-GRPO, CME-GRPO vs. Instruct) isolate CME-GRPO’s gain over same-family checkpoints; vs.-reference comparisons place all three policies on a common quality axis.

We test this directly by holding the generator fixed ( Qwen2.5-0.5B ) and vary-ing the verifier across eight conditions: arandomly initialized control, same-family veri-fiers ( Qwen2.5-0.5B , Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct ,

Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct ), and cross-family veri-fiers spanning 270M to 4B parameters.

Relation to spurious rewards. Concurrent work (Shao et al., 2025) shows that RL fine-tuning of Qwen-Math models can improve from random reward signals, suggesting some label-free gains reflect surface properties rather than learned signal. Our random-verifier control quantifies this effect in the open-ended setting: a random verifier does pro-vide some lift over the base ( 55 .8% vs 50% ), but every real-weighted verifier exceeds it, and gains scale with verifier capability. 

5 Conclusion 

We introduced CME, a label-free reward for RL post-training defined as the negative log-likelihood of a generator’s response under a separate verifier model. CME is a drop-in replacement for the re-ward in GRPO and extends label-free RL to open-ended instruction following. Across four model families and three training regimes, CME-GRPO beats the untrained base in every head-to-head com-parison and matches DPO on SFT backbones using only prompts. Performance scales with verifier capability, and our random-verifier control con-firms the signal comes from cross-model evaluation rather than generic regularization. Limitations. CME requires a verifier held in memory alongside the generator, adding compute and memory overhead. Because the reward mea-sures how unsurprising a response is to the verifier rather than whether it is correct, CME is in prin-ciple vulnerable to drift toward verifier-style out-puts or mode collapse toward the verifier’s prior; we did not observe either failure in our experi-ments, but we did not test long-horizon training where these effects might emerge. Our experiments cover only small generators (0.5B–1.5B parame-ters) and English-language UltraFeedback prompts; behavior at larger scale or in other languages re-mains to be evaluated. We do not run self-entropy GRPO baselines (RENT, EM-RL) directly; our same-family verifier comparison in Section 3 is the closest mechanically applicable analog. Finally, we rely on LLM-as-Judge for evaluation rather than human ratings, which introduces judge-specific bi-ases despite our use of two independent judges with high agreement.

Edge cases. When generator and verifier tokens cover identical spans, Vt contains a single verifier token with weight one. When the generator tokenizer is coarser, verifier tokens fall fully inside generator tokens and contribute their full log-probability (the unweighted sum equals the joint character-span log-probability by the chain rule). When the verifier tokenizer is coarser, fractional weighting prevents the verifier token’s log-probability from being counted multiple times. Generator tokens that decode to whitespace or have no overlapping verifier token are assigned zero aligned log-probability and masked from advantage normalization. 

Worked example. For the response "unhappiness" with generator tokenization ["un", "happiness"] (spans [0 , 2) , [2 , 11) ) and verifier tokenization ["unhappy", "ness"] (spans [0 , 7) ,

[7 , 11) ), the verifier token "unhappy" straddles both generator tokens: 2/7 of its log-probability falls in "un" and 5/7 in "happiness" . The verifier token "ness" sits entirely within "happiness" and con-tributes its full log-probability. The two aligned per-position log-probabilities sum to the total verifier log-likelihood of "unhappiness" .

Why not average? Averaging verifier log-probabilities over Vt would make the aggregate reward depend on tokenization granularity rather than response content: a generator token covering k verifier tokens would receive 1/k of their summed log-probability rather than the full sum. This would also underweight spans the verifier tokenizes finely — exactly the spans where verifier surprise is most informative. 

C Reverse-KL Connection 

The CME reward connects to reverse-KL distillation through the cross-entropy decomposition. For the sequence-level reward r(y) = log πϕ(y | x),

Ey∼πθ [r(y)] = −H(πθ, π ϕ)= −H(πθ) − DKL (πθ ∥ πϕ),

so maximizing expected CME pulls πθ toward πϕ. This identity is exact for the sequence-level reward under the verifier’s tokenization; the token-level variant of Eq. 1, with character-aligned weights and group-normalized per-position advantages, is a heuristic extension that targets the same direction in distribution space without admitting the same closed form. CME-GRPO is thus closely related to MiniLLM (Gu et al., 2024), which optimizes reverse KL between student and teacher via policy gradient on student samples scored by the teacher. The differences are operational: GRPO’s group-normalized advantages depend only on the relative ranking of CME values within a sampled group, and a KL anchor to the reference policy bounds drift toward the verifier. We therefore view CME-GRPO as an RL formulation of on-policy reverse-KL distillation, with the verifier acting as a critic rather than a target distribution. 

D Related Work 

D.1 RLHF and Preference Optimization 

The modern RLHF pipeline was formalized by Ouyang et al. (2022) in InstructGPT, which trained GPT-3 to follow instructions using human preference labels and PPO (Schulman et al., 2017). This established the three-stage recipe—SFT, reward modeling, and RL—that underpinned the first generation of aligned language models. A key limitation of RLHF is the cost of human preference data. Bai et al. (2022) introduced Consti-tutional AI and RLAIF, using an LLM to generate preference labels from written principles. Lee et al. (2023) compared RLAIF to RLHF and found comparable alignment quality, validating AI feedback as a substitute. CME goes further: rather than prompting a model to judge quality, we derive a reward directly from token-level probabilities, requiring no prompting or structured judgment. DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023) eliminated the reward model by re-parameterizing the RLHF objective as a classification loss on preference pairs. DPO is simpler than PPO-based RLHF but requires offline preference pairs. Online variants such as Online DPO and IPO (Azar et al., 2024) address distribution shift by generating fresh completions during training. CME-GRPO is fully online and label-free. 

D.2 Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards 

The insight enabling GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) was that mathematical correctness provides a free, scalable reward: reward 1 if the final answer is correct, else 0. DeepSeek-R1 (Guo et al., 2025) scaled this approach and showed emergent reasoning behaviors including self-reflection without supervised reasoning traces. Subsequent improvements include DAPO (Yu et al., 2025), which introduced decoupled clipping and dynamic sampling for large-scale GRPO stability, and PRIME (Cui et al., 2025), which augments GRPO with process-level reward shaping. All operate in verifiable domains; we extend GRPO to non-verifiable domains by substituting cross-model entropy for the ground-truth verifier. 

D.3 Self-Improvement Without Labels 

STaR (Zelikman et al., 2022) bootstrapped reasoning by iteratively fine-tuning on correct self-generated rationales, requiring only binary answer verification. Self-consistency (Wang et al., 2022) showed that majority voting over reasoning paths improves accuracy at inference time. TTRL (Zuo et al., 2025) converted self-consistency into a training signal, using majority vote as a pseudo-label for GRPO. RL-Without-GT (Xin et al., 2025) showed that format and response length serve as effective surrogate signals for math training. Evol-RL (Zhou et al., 2025) identified “cognitive collapse” in majority-voting methods— models losing solution diversity—and added a semantic novelty reward. MM-UPT (Wei et al., 2025) extended unsupervised GRPO to multimodal LLMs. All of these methods are fundamentally self-referential: the reward derives entirely from the model’s own outputs. If the model is systematically wrong in a way multiple rollouts agree on, the reward reinforces the error. CME-GRPO addresses this by drawing the reward from an external model in a different family. 

D.4 Knowledge Distillation. 

Maximizing expected CME minimizes DKL (πθ ∥ πϕ) on-policy, the reverse-KL objective optimized by MiniLLM (Gu et al., 2024; Hinton et al., 2015). CME-GRPO differs operationally: group-normalized advantages depend only on the relative ranking of CME values within a sampled group, and a KL anchor to the reference policy bounds drift toward the verifier — yielding an RL formulation in which the verifier acts as a critic rather than a target distribution. 

D.5 Cross-Model Likelihood as a Correctness Signal 

Prior work (Gorbett and Jana, 2026) establishes that verifier likelihood ranks correct above incorrect responses across diverse tasks and model families, framed as a passive inference-time correctness signal for evaluation and selection. Our work translates this passive signal into an active training objective: rather than scoring fixed responses, we use verifier likelihood as the reward in policy optimization, and introduce a token-level variant with dense per-position advantages. 

D.6 Process Reward Models 

Outcome reward models (ORMs) assign a single scalar to a complete response, creating sparse credit assignment. Lightman et al. (2023) addressed this with process reward models (PRMs) trained on human-labeled step-level correctness (PRM800K), which consistently outperform ORMs on reasoning tasks. The bottleneck is annotation cost. Math-Shepherd (Wang et al., 2024) automated PRM supervision via Monte Carlo estimation. Luo et al. (2024) scaled this further, and Zheng et al. (2025b) showed LLM-as-a-judge outperforms MC estimation for PRM data quality. Zheng et al. (2025a) surveys PRMs across math, code, multimodal, and agentic domains. The token-level CME variant provides per-token process rewards that are training-free, requiring only verifier forward passes. 

D.7 Reward Signal Reliability and Goodhart’s Law 

Shao et al. (2025) showed that Qwen2.5-Math models improve substantially even with random, format-only, or incorrect reward signals, suggesting RLVR on these models may surface latent pretraining knowledge rather than new reasoning. The effect is model-family-specific: Llama and OLMo models do not improve with spurious rewards. This motivates evaluating CME on both Qwen and non-Qwen generators to ensure results reflect genuine signal quality. Reward hacking more broadly (Skalse et al., 2022; Pan et al., 2022) is mitigated in CME by the cross-family verifier design: the generator cannot exploit biases of a model from a different training lineage as easily as it can exploit its own self-consistency. 

D.8 LLM Evaluation Benchmarks 

Mathematical reasoning benchmarks used in our evaluation include the MATH dataset (Hendrycks et al., 2021), MATH-500 (Lightman et al., 2023), AIME 2024 (Mathematical Association of America, 2024), and AMC 2023. Instruction-following quality is measured via AlpacaEval 2.0 (Dubois et al., 2024), which reports win rate against GPT-4-turbo using an LLM judge. 

E Experiment Details 

Generators and verifier. We fine-tune two base generator models of comparable scale but differ-ent families: Qwen2.5-0.5B (Yang et al., 2024) and Llama-3.2-1B (Meta AI, 2024). The verifier is 

gemma-3-1b-it (Gemma Team, 2025), drawn from a third model family to ensure cross-family CME signal. The verifier weights are kept in bf16 and never updated; they incur only a single forward pass per generated response. 

Training data. We sample a 5,000-instruction subset of UltraFeedback (Cui et al., 2024), retaining only the instruction field and discarding all human preference annotations and chosen/rejected completions. The subset is held fixed across both generator runs. 

Reward and objective. We use the token-level CME from Eq. (1) computed over the entire generator response, with character-level alignment between the generator’s and verifier’s tokenizers (Section ?? ). Per-position advantages are normalized across the group of G = 8 rollouts at each token position, and the CME-GRPO objective is applied with KL anchor coefficient β = 0 and PPO clipping ϵ = 0 .2.

Training hyperparameters. Both generators are trained for one epoch on the 5K subset. Sampling uses temperature T = 1 .0, top-p = 1 , and max response length 2048 tokens. We use AdamW with peak learning rate 3×10 −6 (Qwen) and 5×10 −6 (Llama), linear warmup over 20 steps followed by cosine decay, gradient norm clipped at 1.0, and bf16 mixed precision. The effective batch is 128 prompts per optimizer update (per-device batch 1, G = 8 rollouts, 128 /G = 16 unique prompts × G accumulated micro-steps . . . effective 128 completions). All runs use a single NVIDIA A6000 (48GB). 

Evaluation. Pairwise judge on AlpacaEval 2.0 prompts. We use the 805 -prompt AlpacaEval 2.0 evaluation set (Dubois et al., 2024) as a fixed, diverse held-out instruction distribution. We anchor against 

gemma-3-1b-it , a small but capable instruction-tuned checkpoint comparable in scale to the systems under test. For each generator backbone we generate greedy responses on the full 805 prompts from four 

models—the untrained base policy, our CME-GRPO fine-tune, the same-family instruct checkpoint, and the gemma-3-1b-it reference—and run five pairwise GPT-5.2 judgments per prompt with randomized 

A/B presentation order to mitigate position bias: BASE vs. CME , CME vs. INSTRUCT , and each of the three policies against the gemma-3-1b-it reference. We report tie-adjusted win rate, (wins + 12 ties )/N ,following AlpacaEval convention. The first two comparisons isolate the gain of CME-GRPO relative to the same-family base and instruct checkpoints (controlled by model architecture and pretraining); the three vs.-reference comparisons place all three policies on a common quality axis defined by an external small instruction-tuned model.
