Abstract 

Long-form chain-of-thought reasoning can im-prove LLM performance on complex tasks, but models often continue generating unnec-essary reasoning after a correct answer has emerged. We refer to this behavior as over-thinking. We study this phenomenon from the perspective of GRPO-style reinforcement learning (RL) post-training, framing it as a training-time credit-assignment problem rather than merely a decoding-time stopping problem. In rollouts sampled at the onset of GRPO train-ing, we observe that successful trajectories can exhibit a slightly higher degree of overthink-ing than unsuccessful trajectories for the same prompts. This early imbalance provides a start-ing point for an undesirable feedback loop: be-cause GRPO assigns sequence-level credit, it cannot distinguish the solution-reaching prefix from the unnecessary continuation that length-ens a successful trajectory. Both receive pos-itive update signal, allowing the initial imbal-ance to grow into more severe overthinking during training. To address this issue, we in-troduce Dynamic Rollout Editing (DRE), a training-time intervention for successful trajec-tories that continue thinking after answer emer-gence. DRE preserves the accepted verified prefix, edits the remaining thinking, and prefers the edited trajectory within the same RL group, weakening the preference signal for unneces-sary thinking without penalizing the reasoning needed to reach the answer. Experiments across diverse tasks show the effectiveness of DRE. 

1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) have substantially improved reasoning performance by generating long-form chain-of-thought processes before pro-ducing final responses (Guo et al., 2025; Yang et al., 2025; Meituan LongCat Team, 2025). This paradigm is central to reinforcement learning post-

training, where models explore reasoning trajecto-ries and learn from outcome feedback. However, longer thinking is not always better: generation length and answer quality are not monotonically re-lated (Ghosal et al., 2025; Han et al., 2026). Once a correct answer has appeared, further thinking can increase inference cost and may even move the model away from the correct solution, a phe-nomenon commonly described as overthinking (Sui et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025). Existing work on overthinking largely treats it as a problem of controlling reasoning length: decid-ing when the model should stop or how its think-ing process can be shortened without hurting ac-curacy. Studies of reasoning dynamics identify boundaries such as the reasoning completion point, where useful reasoning gives way to unnecessary thinking (Wu et al., 2026; Su et al., 2025; Wei et al., 2025). Building on this view, early-exit de-coding, RL-based stopping controllers, and length-oriented regularization aim to reduce excessive gen-eration (Yang et al., 2026; Dai et al., 2026; Kimi Team, 2025). While useful, these methods mainly intervene after the policy has learned its reason-ing behavior. They leave open how reinforcement learning may reinforce overthinking after a correct answer is already verifiable. 1

We study this issue in Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) (Shao et al., 2024), a widely used post-training method for reasoning models. GRPO assigns sequence-level rewards and updates the policy using group-relative advantages. This design improves task success, but can also create a credit-assignment problem: a successful trajectory may contain both thinking that leads to the correct answer and unnecessary thinking after the answer has emerged. Since the same positive advantage is applied to the trajectory as a whole, GRPO does not distinguish the reasoning prefix that leads to the verified answer from the later overthinking that only lengthens the trajectory. To examine this credit-assignment problem, we make answer emergence observable through a parser-verifier analysis. For each trajectory, we identify the first point at which a correct answer can be extracted and externally verified, allowing us to compare behavior before and after answer emer-gence. In rollouts sampled early in GRPO training, successful trajectories already show a slight ten-dency to contain more overthinking after answer emergence than unsuccessful trajectories sampled for the same prompts. This initial asymmetry can be amplified by sequence-level updates: when un-necessary thinking is part of a successful trajectory, GRPO may reinforce it together with the think-ing that leads to the answer. As training improves task success, this coupled credit can make over-thinking after answer emergence more persistent, including redundant checking, answer revisits, and repeated verification. This observation motivates a different intervention. Rather than only stopping a trained model earlier, we aim to change the training signal so that task success is less coupled with over-thinking after answer emergence. A simple length penalty may suppress useful thinking, while penal-izing an entire successful trajectory may weaken the prefix needed to solve the problem. An ef-fective intervention should preserve the verified thinking prefix while reducing the preference sig-nal assigned to subsequent overthinking. In this work, we introduce dynamic rollout edit-ing (DRE), a training-time intervention for success-ful trajectories that contain overthinking after an-swer emergence. Figure 1 contrasts vanilla GRPO with DRE: vanilla GRPO can reinforce success-ful trajectories that include overthinking after an-swer emergence, whereas DRE shifts preference toward verified shorter counterparts. For a success-ful trajectory with overthinking after answer emer-gence, DRE constructs an edited counterpart that preserves the verified prefix up to a conservative boundary and replaces the subsequent overthinking. The edited trajectory is then preferred within the same GRPO group, allowing the model to retain a positive training signal for reaching the correct answer while weakening the association between success and overthinking. We further introduce two complementary training strategies: prefix masking avoids directly penalizing the shared verified prefix, while GClip provides a learning signal for edited tokens near the edit point. 

Contributions. (1) We identify overthinking after answer emergence as a training-time credit-assignment problem in GRPO-style post-training. Our analysis shows that sequence-level advantages can mix credit between reasoning that leads to the answer and unnecessary thinking after answer emergence. (2) We propose DRE, a training-time intervention that weakens the association between task success and unnecessary thinking after answer emergence. DRE assigns a higher preference to shorter edited counterparts within the same GRPO group while preserving the verified reasoning pre-fix that reaches the answer. (3) We demonstrate the broad effectiveness of DRE across mathemat-ical reasoning, science QA, and code generation. DRE reduces thinking length while preserving task performance, mainly after answer emergence.

2.1 Problem Formulation 

We formalize overthinking after answer emergence in terms of thinking trajectories. Given a prompt 

x, the model generates a thinking process t fol-lowed by a content process y. We model this generation process as πθ(t, y | x) = πθ(t |

x)πθ(y | x, t ). The resulting trajectory is denoted as τ = ( x, <think> t </think> y).For each trajectory, we split the thinking process into sentences and scan sentence prefixes from left to right. At each prefix, a parser extracts a can-didate answer, and a verifier checks whether the candidate is correct for the given prompt. We de-fine k⋆(τ ) as the first sentence index whose prefix passes this verification check. If no prefix contains a verified answer, we set k⋆(τ ) = Kt(τ ), where 

Kt(τ ) is the number of thinking sentences in τ .Thus, k⋆(τ ) marks the earliest sentence-level point at which a correct answer becomes verifiable in the thinking process. We use this point as an analysis boundary for measuring overthinking after answer emergence, without assuming that every subsequent token is redundant. 

2.2 Preliminary Observations Setting. We train Qwen3-4B-Base with GRPO following the DeepSeek-R1-Zero (Guo et al., 2025) setting, using only rule-based rewards for answer correctness and format. This setup allows us to examine whether overthinking after answer emer-gence increases during RL training. For each rollout in which a verified answer ap-pears, k⋆(τ ) denotes the first sentence index at which the prefix contains that answer. We count keywords related to verification and checking in the sentences before and after k⋆(τ ). Keywords in sentences before k⋆(τ ) may reflect useful checking before the answer is reached, whereas keywords in sentences after k⋆(τ ) serve as indicators of pos-sible overthinking, since the model continues to check or verify after the answer has already ap-peared. We then examine whether both quantities increase during training. To compare successful and unsuccessful roll-outs for the same prompt, we use answer revisits as a rollout-level measure, because unsuccessful rollouts may not contain a valid k⋆(τ ). For each rollout with an extractable final answer, we count how often that answer is repeated after its first oc-currence, and compare this count across rollouts sampled for the same prompt. 

Observation: RL training improves accuracy while amplifying overthinking behaviors such as answer repetition and redundant verification. 

Figure 2 (a) illustrates the accuracy gain during training. Figure 2 (b) shows that verification and checking keywords increase both before and after 

k⋆(τ ). The increase before k⋆(τ ) is consistent with more useful checking before the verified answer appears. The increase after k⋆(τ ) suggests more unnecessary overthinking after the answer has al-ready emerged. Figure 2 (c) presents a similar pat-tern within GRPO sampling groups: for the same prompt, successful rollouts repeat their extracted answers more often than unsuccessful rollouts. A qualitative example in Appendix 7 illustrates this pattern. At an earlier training step, the model still requires additional thinking to reach the correct an-swer; at a later step, it reaches the verified answer but continues with an additional verification step. 3 Dynamic Rollout Editing 

The analysis in Section 2 motivates our interven-tion: when overthinking after a verified answer is correlated with positive advantage, trajectory-level rewards can reinforce it through the same gradient signal. We address this issue with dynamic roll-out editing , which constructs an edited counterpart for an overthinking rollout within the same GRPO group and assigns higher preference to the accepted edit than to the original. The edit preserves the veri-fied prefix, ends the thinking process at an accepted editable boundary, and reduces preference for over-thinking beyond that boundary. 

3.1 Edited Rollout Synthesis 

For each rollout, dynamic rollout editing searches near answer emergence for an editable boundary, proposes a trajectory that stops thinking there, and accepts it only if both prefix verification and regenerated-content verification succeed. Accepted edits preserve the verified prefix, remove later thinking, and regenerate the content process; failed edits fall back to an unedited copy. 

Editable Boundary Identification. The answer-emergence boundary k⋆ marks the earliest prefix from which the parser-verifier extracts the correct answer, but it is not necessarily a safe edit point: the answer may appear inside an unfinished calculation, provisional statement, or incomplete final-answer pattern. We therefore use k⋆ as the starting point for prefix verification, not as the edit point itself. Starting from k⋆, we append \nFinal Answer

3.2 Edited Rollout Scoring 

The scoring rule turns accepted edits into prefer-ences within the group. For each sampled original trajectory τi, the GRPO group contains one auxil-iary trajectory: the accepted edit ˜τi when both pre-fix verification and verification of the regenerated content succeed, and an unedited copy otherwise. Let V (ρ) = 1 indicate that the content process of trajectory ρ is verified, and let E(ρ) = 1 indicate that ρ is an accepted edit. The scoring rule is a shaped preference score for the expanded GRPO group, not a calibrated task reward. It encodes an additional preference among verified trajectories: when an accepted edit preserves the verified an-swer while removing continuation after ˆk, the edit should be preferred over the original continuation.

The prefer-ence between an accepted edit and a verified origi-nal is not a claim that the edit is more correct; both pass the verifier. It is reward shaping that adds a training preference for stopping after a conser-vative verified boundary. Because the scores are normalized within the group to form advantages, their absolute scale is not calibrated; what matters is the induced within-group ranking and spacing. When the original is also verified, the edit iso-lates the preference for removing post-boundary continuation while preserving the same verified answer. When the original is not verified but the edit is accepted, the edit is best interpreted as cor-recting answer drift after a verified prefix. This case changes both correctness and length, whereas verified-original edits isolate the preference for re-moving post-boundary continuation. 

3.3 Training with Edited Rollouts 

This shaped preference changes the GRPO training signal without redefining task correctness: it weak-ens positive credit for the original thinking after the verified answer boundary. For accepted edit pairs, this preference reversal creates two optimization issues during training, i.e. ,

(1) Negative credit on the shared prefix. The original trajectory receives a lower preference score even though it shares the verified prefix with the edited trajectory. Without a safeguard, the resulting lower advantage would be applied to the entire orig-inal trajectory, including both the accepted verified 5prefix and the overthinking after ˆk.

(2) Upper clipping on edited tokens. The edited suffix begins with the verified final answer string and regenerated content rather than the origi-nal rollout continuation. These positively preferred tokens can have low probability under the old pol-icy near ˆk, so ordinary clipping can remove the ascent signal where the edit needs to be learned. 

Prefix Masking for Preserving the Verified Pre-fix. For an accepted pair (τ, ˜τ ), the original and edited trajectories share the same generated prefix through ˆk. After this prefix, the edited rollout be-gins the verified final answer string, whereas the original keeps thinking after ˆk. Prefix masking applies only to the original trajectory: it excludes the generated tokens in this shared prefix from that trajectory’s GRPO loss, while keeping unit weight on tokens after the divergence. The edited trajec-tory and all other trajectories keep unit weights. Thus, negative relative credit from the original tar-gets its post-ˆk continuation rather than the accepted verified prefix shared with the edit. 

GClip for Learning from Edited Rollouts. 

GClip handles the second issue by using a clipped ratio operator with a custom backward pass for edited tokens. Accepted edits are synthetic con-tinuations constructed after rollout sampling, so the expanded-group objective should be read as a GRPO-style preference surrogate over original and edited trajectories rather than as an unbiased on-policy GRPO estimator. Because the edited suffix no longer follows the original rollout path, its early tokens can have very low old-policy probability near ˆk. Positively preferred edited to-kens can then hit the upper clip boundary quickly, causing ordinary clipping to remove the ascent sig-nal where the edit needs to be learned. 4.2 Main Results 

As shown in Table 1, we make four empirical ob-servations: (1) DRE substantially reduces think-ing tokens while preserving benchmark accu-racy. Among methods with complete LCB-V6 results, DRE has the lowest five-benchmark av-erage thinking length and the highest token effi-ciency in every model block, using only 72.1%, 77.7%, and 75.3% of Raw Model thinking tokens on the three backbones. On Qwen3-4B-Thinking-2507 and Qwen3-8B, this reduction is accompanied by higher average accuracy than both Raw Model and GRPO. On Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking-2507, where all trained variants trail Raw Model, DRE stays closest to Raw Model in accuracy while using the fewest thinking tokens among trained methods. The largest-model result should therefore be read as stronger length control with competitive accuracy, rather than as a uniform accuracy gain. (2) The reduction is not confined to the math training distribution. Although all trainable methods use DAPO-Math-17k, DRE shortens thinking across held-out AIME evaluations, GPQA-D, and out-of-domain LCB-V6. Relative to GRPO, DRE uses fewer thinking tokens on all nine AIME model– benchmark rows, all three GPQA-D rows, and all three LCB-V6 rows. Its accuracy also matches or exceeds GRPO on seven AIME rows, improves GPQA-D accuracy in each model block, and stays within 0.6 accuracy points of the best completed method on LCB-V6. This cross-benchmark pattern is consistent with reducing redundant post-solution continuation rather than only pruning math-specific thinking. (3) Generic length pressure does not reproduce the same pattern. GRPO + LP some-times shortens thinking relative to GRPO, but it remains longer than DRE on the five-benchmark av-erage and yields less stable accuracy. 5 Related Work 

Prior work reduces overthinking by controlling reasoning length during post-training, shortening inference-time generation, or explicitly deciding when to stop (Sui et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025). Post-training methods often use short or variable-length thinking supervision, length rewards or penalties, or latent reasoning schemes (Yu et al., 2024; Kimi Team, 2025; Aggarwal and Welleck, 2025; Hao et al., 2025). Inference-time methods instead shorten an already trained policy through prompts, decoding controls, early-exit signals, or stopping controllers (Yang et al., 2026; Dai et al., 2026; Wei et al., 2025). Our work is complemen-tary: we analyze how GRPO-style credit assign-ment can reinforce overthinking after answer emer-gence, and use dynamic rollout editing to preserve necessary reasoning while reducing preference for unnecessary thinking after the answer becomes ver-ifiable. Appendix A gives a fuller discussion. 

6 Conclusion 

We study why RL-trained reasoning models con-tinue thinking after a correct answer has already emerged. Our analysis suggests that, in GRPO-style training, successful rollouts can contain more overthinking than unsuccessful rollouts sampled for the same prompts, and sequence-level rewards may assign positive credit to both reaching the an-swer and overthinking after it. In this work, we 8propose DRE, which prefers verified shorter trajec-tories within the same RL group while preserving the reasoning prefix that reaches the answer. Exper-iments on diverse benchmarks covering mathemat-ical reasoning, science QA, and code generation validate the effectiveness of DRE. The results show that DRE reduces thinking length while preserving overall task performance, and that this reduction occurs mainly after answer emergence. 

Limitations 

RL training in this work is conducted only on DAPO-Math-17K (Yu et al., 2025), a dataset of math problems with verifiable answers. This set-ting supports reliable identification of answer emer-gence, which is required by dynamic rollout editing, but it also limits the diversity of training signals used to learn shorter reasoning behavior. Although the resulting model reduces thinking tokens on out-of-domain code-generation benchmarks, the gains are smaller than those observed on math tasks. Ex-tending dynamic rollout editing to more diverse verifiable RL training data remains an important di-rection for improving and assessing token savings across domains. 

Ethical Considerations 

This work aims to reduce overthinking after answer emergence in RL-trained reasoning models. The main potential benefit is improved inference effi-ciency, which can lower compute cost and associ-ated resource use. Since shorter thinking processes may not be equally suitable for all tasks, deploy-ments should be preceded by evaluation in the in-tended target domain, especially when additional verification steps are important for reliability.

A Related Work 

We organize related work according to the stage at which prior methods intervene to control reasoning length: during post-training, at inference through prompting or decoding, or via explicit early-exit mechanisms. This organization follows recent tax-onomies of efficient reasoning (Sui et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025). 

Post-training methods. Training-time ap-proaches control reasoning length by modifying supervision signals, reward functions, or the latent reasoning space. Representative directions include supervised fine-tuning with variable-length chain-of-thought data (Yu et al., 2024; Kang et al., 2025; Xia et al., 2025; Ma et al., 2025), RL objectives with explicit length rewards or penalties (Kimi Team, 2025; Luo et al., 2025; Aggarwal and Welleck, 2025), and latent-space reasoning schemes (Hao et al., 2025; Cheng and Durme, 2024; Shen et al., 2025). The proposed method also intervenes during post-training, but differs in mechanism: rather than globally rewarding concise reasoning or requiring short chain-of-thought supervision, it edits high-reward RL rollouts at the point where a verified solution first appears. 

Prompt-based methods. Prompting strategies elicit shorter or more disciplined chains of thought by instructing the model to be concise, decisive, or self-monitoring (Han et al., 2025; Muennighoff et al., 2025; Lee et al., 2025; OpenAI, 2025; Kang et al., 2025; Peng et al., 2025; Huang et al., 2026a). These methods are appealing because they require no retraining, but they tend to be brittle: their ef-fectiveness depends heavily on prompt wording, and a single prompting policy often generalizes poorly across tasks with different reasoning depths or answer formats. 

Early-exit methods. Early-exit approaches inter-vene directly in the generation process by termi-nating reasoning once the model appears to have reached a solution. Some methods rely on exter-nal monitors or additional training, such as auxil-iary classifiers (Liu and Wang, 2025), bandit con-trollers (Sun et al., 2025), difficulty estimators (Pu et al., 2025), RL-based stopping policies such as S-GRPO (Dai et al., 2026), or self-aware guided sam-pling as in SAGE-RL (Huang et al., 2026b). Others pursue lower-overhead stopping rules based on in-trinsic model signals, such as DEER (Yang et al., 2026), or are motivated by reasoning-dynamics analyses that characterize an instance-specific post-convergence regime via a reasoning completion point (Wei et al., 2025). The present work is com-plementary to this line of research: rather than stopping generation through a controller or sam-pling rule, it edits sampled rollouts after verified answer emergence and trains against the later con-tinuation. 

B Experimental Details 

Training data and rollout sampling. All training-time variants are trained on DAPO-Math-17k (Yu et al., 2025). During training, we use a maximum generation length of 28,000 tokens. Each optimization step uses a batch size of 64 prompts, and each prompt contributes an effec-tive group of 8 trajectories or candidates for group-relative normalization. For GRPO and GRPO + LP, these are 8 sampled rollouts. For DRE, we sample 4 original rollouts and construct 4 auxiliary coun-terparts; each auxiliary trajectory is an accepted edit when both prefix verification and verification of the regenerated content process succeed, and an unedited copy otherwise. For S-GRPO, we sam-ple 1 rollout and construct 8 candidates from that rollout following its stopping-policy construction. 

Training horizon and checkpoint selection. 

For DRE, we select the first checkpoint whose validation-set mean full thinking length is shorter than the raw model’s validation-set mean k⋆ to-ken position. This stopping rule selects the point at which the trained model’s complete thinking process has become shorter than the raw model’s answer-emergence boundary on the validation set. For the other trained methods, we train to 100 opti-mization steps and report the step-100 checkpoint. In S-GRPO runs that collapse before step 100 on a training set, we instead report the last checkpoint 11 before collapse and do not use post-collapse check-points. 

Optimization hyperparameters. We do not use a KL penalty in any training variant, i.e., the KL co-efficient is set to zero. For ordinary clipped GRPO objectives, including the GRPO, GRPO + LP, S-GRPO, and Standard Clip settings, we use asym-metric ratio clipping with lower-side ϵ↓ = 0 .20

C Ablations 

We report leave-one-out ablations evaluated on selected AIME24 samples across training steps. The Full Method combines prefix verification, boundary-based editing, prefix masking, and GClip.

These ablations test whether each safeguard is needed to reduce overthinking while preserving task performance. F Edited-Suffix Distribution Mismatch 

The Standard Clip ablation in Appendix C is moti-vated by the local distribution mismatch introduced when an accepted training-time edit replaces the original continuation after the accepted editable boundary ˆk. The edited suffix no longer follows the sampled trajectory under πθold , so its early to-kens can receive low old-policy probability near the boundary. Figure 6 shows a representative ac-cepted pair over the first 80 suffix positions; the shaded cue region starts at ˆk and includes the in-serted final-answer cue. The original suffix remains on the original sampled trajectory, whereas the edited suffix begins with the verified final-answer string that closes the thinking process, followed by the regenerated content process. This mismatch can make positively preferred edited tokens more likely to enter upper-side clip saturation once their current-policy probability increases, which is the case where GClip preserves straight-through learn-ing signal. This mismatch also clarifies the status of the ob-jective. The edited suffix is deliberately introduced as a verified synthetic alternative to the original continuation. Consequently, the edited-trajectory update should not be read as satisfying the usual on-policy sampling assumptions behind PPO-style clipping. Instead, DRE uses the clipped-ratio form as a bounded forward surrogate for a preference constructed from verifier-accepted edits. 15 20 0 20 40 60 80

Following the reasoning-dynamics analysis of LLM overthink-ing (Wei et al., 2025), we examine whether DRE shortens post-solution continuation after answer emergence without erasing thinking that may be needed before the answer-emergence boundary k⋆.For each thinking prefix k, we force the thinking process to stop at that prefix, sample content con-tinuations, embed the sampled content processes, and average them into a prefix-level centroid ek.The left panel projects these centroids into a shared two-dimensional PCA space for visualization only. In contrast, the DRE-trained model reaches a compact region over a shorter prefix range. Because each residual curve uses its own post-k⋆ centroid, the residual values should be read as within-trajectory concentration rather than distances to a shared semantic target.
