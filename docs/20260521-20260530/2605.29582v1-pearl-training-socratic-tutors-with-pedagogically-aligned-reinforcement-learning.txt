# PEARL: Training Socratic Tutors with Pedagogically Aligned Reinforcement Learning 

Qikai Chang 1, Zhenrong Zhang 1,2 , Linbo Chen 1, Pengfei Hu 1,Jianshu Zhang 2, Youhui Guo 2, Jun Du 1*1University of Science and Technology of China, 2iFLYTEK Research 

Abstract 

Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown promise as educational tutors, yet effective tu-toring requires more than solving problems: it must provide progressive Socratic guidance and balance multiple pedagogical objectives across multi-turn interactions. However, train-ing such tutors remains challenging due to limited-fidelity and weakly controllable stu-dent simulation, under-specified pedagogical reward modeling, and unstable multi-objective optimization. To overcome these limitations, we propose PEARL, a pe dagogically aligned 

reinforcement learning framework for training Socratic tutoring agents, consisting of three key components. First, we introduce a con-trollable student simulator that decouples la-tent cognitive states from response generation to model diverse abilities and misconceptions. Second, we develop a generative reward model that jointly evaluates pedagogical quality and objective correctness for policy optimization. Finally, we propose a stable multi-objective RL scheme that discretizes rewards within each dimension and aggregates normalized ad-vantages across dimensions, preventing high-variance objectives from dominating updates. Experiments on multiple benchmarks show that PEARL achieves the best performance among open-source models and remains competitive with leading proprietary LLMs, despite using only a 30B policy model 1.

Although recent studies have made meaningful progress in AI tutors (Shi et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2025b; Wang et al., 2025), training high-quality tutoring agents with RL still faces three core bottle-necks: limited-fidelity student simulation, under-specified pedagogical reward modeling, and un-stable multi-objective optimization . (1) For stu-dent simulation , existing student simulators typi-cally prompt LLMs to role-play students (Macina et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024a; Dinucu-Jianu et al., 2025), sometimes with additional student profiles for diversity (Liu et al., 2024b; Gao et al., 2025). However, they usually generate responses end-to-end without explicitly modeling students’ cogni-tive states or decision-making processes, limiting their ability to capture diverse misconceptions, par-tial understandings, and evolving learning dynam-ics, which creates a fidelity gap for tutor training. (2) For pedagogical reward modeling , existing evaluations either rely on costly human annota-tion (Macina et al., 2023) or adopt LLM-as-a-judge frameworks (Wei et al., 2025; Song et al., 2025). However, these signals are often insufficient for RL optimization: they lack fine-grained trajectory-level scoring criteria, and rarely jointly assess peda-gogical effectiveness and objective correctness. (3) For multi-objective optimization , pedagogical tu-toring involves multiple competing objectives, yet prior approaches often compute terminal rewards via simple averaging (Song et al., 2025). Because evaluation metrics differ substantially in variance scales and reward sparsity, high-variance objec-tives can dominate gradient updates under policy-gradient methods such as GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) and PPO (Schulman et al., 2017), hindering bal-anced optimization across instructional goals. To address these challenges, we propose PEARL, a pe dagogically aligned reinforcement learning framework for training Socratic tutoring agents. PEARL enhances the tutoring capability of LLMs through three components: simulation environment design, reward modeling, and training algorithms. (1) For student simulation , we design a control-lable student simulator with cognition-decision de-coupling that explicitly models students’ learning abilities. By separating cognitive priors from the generation process, it enables more diverse and controllable simulation of student misconceptions and learning dynamics, reducing fidelity gaps. (2) For pedagogical reward modeling , we develop a generative reward model for tutoring dialogue assessment. It jointly evaluates pedagogical effec-tiveness and objective correctness, while produc-ing interpretable rationales and reward signals for RL optimization. (3) For multi-objective opti-mization , we propose a stable multi-objective RL scheme that discretizes rewards within each dimen-sion to denoise noisy signals, and aggregates nor-malized advantages across dimensions to prevent high-variance objectives from dominating updates. We evaluate PEARL on multiple benchmarks across diverse educational difficulty levels, includ-ing GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), MATH-500 (Hendrycks et al., 2021), MathTutorBench (Macina et al., 2025), and MathDial (Macina et al., 2023). Experimental results show that PEARL, despite using only a 30B policy model, achieves the best performance among open-source models and re-mains competitive with proprietary LLMs. Our primary contributions are as follows: • We propose a cognition-decision decoupled student simulator that explicitly models stu-dents’ learning ability, enabling controllable simulation of student learning behaviors. • We develop a generative reward model that turns pedagogical principles into executable reward criteria, jointly capturing pedagogical effectiveness and objective correctness. • We introduce a multi-objective RL optimiza-tion scheme that combines reward discretiza-tion and advantage aggregation to achieve bal-anced optimization across multiple objectives. • Experiments on multiple benchmarks demon-strate that PEARL achieves the best perfor-mance among open-source models.

2.2 Controllable Student Simulation 

Effective tutor training requires interaction with students exhibiting diverse abilities, misconcep-tions, and learning behaviors. However, existing LLM-based role-playing simulators encode student characteristics only implicitly, leading to limited controllability and weak cognitive grounding. To address this, we introduce explicit cognition mod-eling: instead of directly generating student re-sponses, our simulator first represents what the student knows and how the student tends to rea-son, and then uses these latent cognitive states to guide response generation. Specifically, we decou-ple student simulation into knowledge decomposi-tion, latent cognitive state modeling, and response generation, as illustrated in Figure 2. 

Knowledge Decomposition. Given a problem q,we decompose it into a set of structured knowledge units, such as integer arithmetic and equation solv-ing, and organize them into a knowledge network according to prerequisite relations. This network provides an explicit representation of the knowl-edge structure required for solving the problem. For a student at turn t, we denote the mastery states over these units as mt. By assigning different mas-tery states to different students, the simulator can explicitly control variations in knowledge mastery. 

Latent Cognitive State Modeling. Beyond what the student knows, effective simulation also re-quires modeling how the student engages with tu-toring. We therefore introduce a latent cognitive profile c to characterize student-level cognitive and behavioral tendencies, including activeness, learn-ing perseverance, comprehension ability, expres-sive ability, and attention. During multi-turn inter-actions, the mastery state mt is dynamically up-dated according to the problem, dialogue context, 3tutor action, and student response:

Overall, by explicitly modeling student cogni-tion before response generation, our simulator pro-vides a controllable and cognitively grounded envi-ronment for training Socratic tutoring agents. 

2.3 Generative Reward Modeling 

After constructing a controllable student interaction environment, training an effective Socratic tutor re-quires reward signals that capture not only final correctness, but also the quality of multi-turn guid-ance, answer-leakage avoidance, and pedagogical scaffolding (Team et al., 2025b, 2024). Motivated by learning-science-based rubrics for AI tutoring, we design a generative reward model that turns high-level pedagogical principles into executable trajectory-level reward criteria with fine-grained scoring standards. We further extend prior peda-gogical categories with task-grounded dimensions, including tutoring accuracy, answer-leakage con-trol, and tutoring completeness, and condition the judge on student cognitive states to assess learner-specific pedagogical appropriateness. Given a complete tutoring trajectory τ , the judge model πjudge evaluates the tutor’s performance con-ditioned on the problem, reference solution, and student cognitive states reflected throughout the interaction. Conditioning on cognitive states al-lows the judge to assess whether the tutor’s behav-ior is pedagogically appropriate for the student’s evolving understanding, rather than evaluating the dialogue independently of the student’s current un-derstanding. We define the reward over several pedagogical dimensions, including tutoring accu-racy, answer-leakage control, tutoring complete-ness, cognitive load management, Socratic guid-ance, metacognitive facilitation, adaptive teaching, and emotional support. Together, these dimensions capture three complementary aspects of tutoring quality: instructional correctness, pedagogical pro-cess quality, and affective support. Formally, let 

K = {acc , leak , complete , load ,

guide , meta , adaptive , emotion }, (6) denote the set of reward dimensions. For each dimension k ∈ K , we define a set of dimension-specific scoring criteria Jk, where each criterion 

j ∈ J k outputs a discrete score ˆrk,j (τ ). Since in-complete tutoring trajectories should not receive positive pedagogical rewards, we first derive a bi-nary completion indicator g(τ ) ∈ { 0, 1} from the completion dimension, where g(τ ) = 0 indicates that the tutoring process is incomplete. 3 Experiments 

3.1 Datasets 

We evaluate the proposed method on several representative benchmarks, including GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) for middle-school mathemat-ics, MATH-500 (Hendrycks et al., 2021) for high-school mathematics, and the education-oriented benchmarks MathTutorBench (Macina et al., 2025) and MathDial (Macina et al., 2023). Together, these datasets cover a diverse range of mathematical do-mains, such as set theory, algebra, and number the-ory. The quality of tutoring interactions is compre-hensively assessed using the trained judge model. Detailed implementation settings for the judge, tu-tor, and student agents are provided in Appendix D. 

3.2 Comparison with State-of-the-Art Methods 

To evaluate the effectiveness of PEARL, we con-duct a systematic comparison with a diverse set of open-source and proprietary LLMs, together with recent tutoring-oriented systems, including GPT-5.1, Gemini-3-Flash, MuduoLLM-14B (Zhu et al., 2025), InnoSpark (Song et al., 2025), DeepSeek-V3.2 (Liu et al., 2025a), Kimi-K2-Instruct (Team et al., 2025a), GLM-4.7 (Zeng et al., 2025), MiniMax-M2.1 (Chen et al., 2025), Qwen3-235B-A22B (Yang et al., 2025), and our baseline model, Qwen3-30B-A3B (Yang et al., 2025). As shown in Table 1, after multi-stage training, PEARL achieves substantial improvements over the baseline across multiple evaluation dimensions, demonstrating that the proposed optimization framework effectively enhances the tutor’s pedagogical ability. Notably, although PEARL relies on a relatively small 30B policy model, it remains competitive with cur-rent state-of-the-art systems and even outperforms many substantially larger open-source models.

The results show that, although our reward model is built on a 32B backbone, it still achieves strong agreement with Gemini-3-Pro. 4 Conclusion 

In this work, we address three core challenges in training LLM-based Socratic tutors: high-fidelity student simulation, well-specified pedagogical re-ward modeling, and stable multi-objective opti-mization. To this end, we propose PEARL, a uni-fied framework for training pedagogically aligned tutoring agents. First, we introduce a controllable student simulator with cognition-decision decou-pling to improve the fidelity, diversity, and con-trollability of tutoring interactions. Second, we develop a generative reward model that instantiates pedagogical principles into reward criteria, jointly 8evaluating pedagogical quality and objective cor-rectness. Finally, to stabilize learning under mul-tiple objectives, we propose a multi-objective RL scheme based on reward discretization, turn-count penalty, and advantage aggregation. Experiments on multiple benchmarks show that PEARL yields substantial improvements and achieves competitive tutoring performance with only a 30B model. 

Limitations 

This work has several limitations. First, although the proposed student simulator improves controlla-bility and interaction fidelity, it cannot fully capture the complexity of real students in authentic educa-tional settings. In particular, the current framework focuses on short-horizon tutoring interactions and does not explicitly model long-term learning dy-namics. In future work, we plan to explore the long-term evolution of student behavior and de-velop memory-based mechanisms for long-horizon tutoring. Second, our experiments mainly focus on mathematical tutoring, and the generalizability of PEARL to other educational domains remains to be validated. We plan to extend the framework to a broader range of subjects and tasks, such as language learning, physics, and chemistry. Third, although our generative reward model shows strong agreement with expert preferences, it is trained from model-generated annotations and may inherit biases from the annotator model. Future work should incorporate broader human annotations and examine whether reward improvements translate into long-term learning gains for real students. 

Ethical Considerations 

This work studies LLM-based Socratic tutoring in simulated environments and does not use private student data. Human expert annotations are used only for preference evaluation and do not involve personally identifiable information. We inspect the generated tutoring data to remove potential per-sonal identifiers or inappropriate content. Since LLM tutors may still produce incorrect or biased guidance, real-world deployment should require human oversight and appropriate safeguards.

B Related Work 

LLMs have shown strong potential in education. In this section, we review three lines of work most relevant to training effective tutoring agents: stu-dent simulation, tutoring dialogue evaluation, and pedagogical alignment for LLM tutors. 

B.1 LLM Tutors and Student Simulation 

Recent studies have shown that LLMs hold strong promise for personalized learning. Early work mainly relied on prompt engineering to elicit So-cratic tutoring behaviors (Park et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024), demonstrating their ability to provide both answers and multi-turn tutoring. More recent systems further emphasize pedagogically grounded interaction, including scaf-folded prompting, student-centered dialogue, and multi-turn learning support (Dinucu-Jianu et al., 2025; Scarlatos et al., 2025; Shi et al., 2025). Student simulation is also important for tutor de-sign, as exposure to students with diverse cognitive states helps tutors adapt feedback and interaction strategies to individual learners (Villegas-Ch et al., 2025). Prior work has explored simulating learners with different personality traits (Liu et al., 2024b), using LLMs for personalized error correction (Da-heim et al., 2024), fine-tuning LLMs on diverse student data (Sonkar et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2025), and incorporating learner profiles into tutoring sys-tems (Park et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2025). However, most existing methods still rely on end-to-end re-sponse generation, which limits controllability. In contrast, we propose a controllable student simu-lator with cognition-decision decoupling for more faithful and controllable learning dynamics. 

B.2 Tutoring Dialogue Evaluation 

Task-specific evaluation typically relies on fixed question-answer settings to assess domain knowl-edge and reasoning ability, such as GPQA (Rein et al., 2024), MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020), MMLU-Pro (Wang et al., 2024), and MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021). Evaluating tutoring ability, however, is more challenging, as it must jointly account for pedagogical quality and task correctness over multi-turn interactions. Early stud-ies, therefore, often relied on human annotation (Macina et al., 2023), which is difficult to scale. With the rise of LLM-as-a-judge (Zheng et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2024), recent work has introduced automated benchmarks and model-based evalua-tors for tutoring assessment (Macina et al., 2025; Maurya et al., 2025; Wei et al., 2025). He et al. (2024) use Socratic prompting for reference-free reasoning evaluation with LLMs. However, ex-isting methods often remain insufficient for tutor optimization: they provide limited fine-grained, trajectory-level judgment rules, rarely unify peda-gogical quality with objective correctness, and are not directly designed to produce reward signals for RL. 

B.3 Pedagogical Alignment for LLM Tutors 

Pedagogical alignment is central to training effec-tive LLM tutors. Existing approaches mainly in-clude supervised fine-tuning (Bonino et al., 2024), preference optimization (e.g., DPO), and RL meth-ods such as PPO (Schulman et al., 2017), GRPO (Shao et al., 2024), DAPO (Yu et al., 2025), and GSPO (Zheng et al., 2025). In educational settings, Scarlatos et al. (2025) applied DPO to improve student outcomes, Dinucu-Jianu et al. (2025) used online RL to balance answer withholding and ped-agogical correctness, and Song et al. (2025) used GRPO to optimize dimensions such as helpfulness, personalization, and creativity. Jiang et al. (2025) combine evolutionary search with RL to optimize Socratic tutoring policies. However, tutoring is in-herently multi-objective, and different objectives often exhibit markedly different variance scales and sparsity patterns. Existing methods typically com-bine them through simple linear averaging (Song et al., 2025), which can cause high-variance objec-tives to dominate updates and destabilize training, especially in multi-turn dialogues. 

C Tutor Evaluation Metrics 

We evaluate tutoring dialogues from the perspec-tives of both objective correctness and pedagogical quality. Given the problem, reference answer, solu-tion, student profile, and the full tutor-student dia-logue, the judge agent assesses the tutor along eight dimensions: Acc , Leak , Complete , Load , Guide ,12 Meta , Adaptive , and Emotion .

C.1 Accuracy (Acc) 

This metric evaluates whether the tutor’s teaching process is mathematically and logically correct. The judge checks the correctness of the tutor’s ex-planations, calculations, step-by-step guidance, and feedback to the student. A low score is assigned if the tutor makes factual, computational, or rea-soning errors, or fails to guide the student to the reference answer. 

C.2 Answer-Leakage Control (Leak) 

This metric evaluates whether the tutor reveals the answer or replaces the student’s core reasoning pro-cess. Full leakage refers to directly giving the final answer or key calculation results. Partial leakage occurs when the tutor provides overly specific hints or candidate answers that reduce the task to sim-ple verification. No leakage means that the tutor uses open-ended questions, general strategies, or conceptual scaffolding while leaving the essential reasoning to the student. 

C.3 Completion (Complete) 

This metric evaluates whether the tutor com-pletes the instructional process. The judge checks whether the tutor covers the necessary reasoning steps, addresses student mistakes, helps the student reach the correct answer, and provides an appro-priate conclusion. Dialogues that stop too early or miss key steps receive lower scores. 

C.4 Cognitive Load Management (Load) 

This metric measures whether the tutor presents information in a clear and manageable way. It in-cludes four aspects: whether the explanation is concise, whether the information is clear and easy to understand, whether the response length is ap-propriate, and whether the tutor avoids irrelevant information. 

C.5 Socratic Guidance (Guide) 

This metric evaluates whether the tutor promotes active student participation. It includes the quality of the tutor’s questioning strategy, the effectiveness of the provided guidance, and whether the tutor encourages the student to remain the main problem solver rather than passively following instructions. 

C.6 Metacognition (Meta) 

This metric evaluates whether the tutor encourages the student to reflect on their reasoning. It includes whether the tutor guides the student to discover mistakes, prompts the student to explain or verify their thinking, and summarizes the core method or insight after the problem is solved. 

C.7 Adaptivity (Adaptive) 

This metric evaluates whether the tutor adapts to the student’s learning state. It includes whether the tutor can help the student when they are stuck by changing strategies, and whether the tutor adjusts question difficulty according to the student’s re-sponses, ability, and demonstrated understanding. 

C.8 Affective Support (Emotion) 

This metric evaluates whether the tutor creates a positive and supportive learning environment. It considers whether the tutor maintains an encourag-ing tone and whether student mistakes are handled as learning opportunities rather than failures. 

D Implementation Details 

D.1 Test Setting 

During inference, we allow at most 20 interaction turns between the tutor agent and the student agent, with a maximum generation length of 8,192 tokens. For both agents, we set the sampling temperature to 1.0, top-p to 1.0, and top-k to 50. 

D.2 Judge Agent 

To improve both interpretability and evaluation reli-ability, we train the judge model under a generative evaluation paradigm. Specifically, we collect di-verse tutoring trajectories generated by different tutor and student models, and use Gemini-3-Pro to annotate each trajectory with dimension-specific scores and corresponding natural-language ratio-nales. After filtering out noisy and inconsistent annotations, we obtain 803K training instances for supervising πjudge .We fully fine-tune the judge agent from Qwen3-32B (Yang et al., 2025) for 1 epoch, using a global batch size of 256. We adopt AdamW (Loshchilov and Hutter, 2017) as the optimizer and fix the learn-ing rate at 5×10 −6. To improve memory efficiency, we employ ZeRO (Rajbhandari et al., 2020) during training. At inference time, to reduce output vari-ance, we set the sampling temperature to 0.3, top-p

to 1.0, and top-k to 20. 13 D.3 Tutor Agent 

In the cold-start stage, we use DeepSeek-V3.2 (Liu et al., 2025a) to interact with the student simulator over multiple turns and generate high-quality tutoring dialogues. After applying data filtering strategies such as turn balancing, we con-struct the cold-start dataset D, which contains 193K samples. Based on this dataset, we conduct full-parameter fine-tuning on Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct-2507 (Yang et al., 2025) for one epoch, us-ing AdamW with a fixed learning rate of 5 × 10 −6.In the reinforcement learning stage, we optimize the tutor model using GSPO. Specifically, the group size G is set to 8, the sampling temperature is set to 1.0, and the learning rate is set to 1 × 10 −6, without KL regularization. The clipping coefficients are set to ϵhigh = 4 × 10 −4 and ϵlow = 3 × 10 −4. The turn-count decay factor γ is set to 0.98. During rollout, the maximum generation length is 6,144 tokens, and the maximum number of interaction turns is 15. All experiments are conducted on 8 NVIDIA H200 GPUs. 

D.4 Student Agent 

The student simulator is also implemented based on Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct-2507 (Yang et al., 2025). Benefiting from the proposed cognition-decoupling mechanism, the simulator can effectively capture diverse student behaviors, eliminating the need for dedicated fine-tuning of the student model. 

D.5 Prompts 

The complete prompt settings used in our method, including those for the Student Agent, Tutor Agent, and Judge Agent, are available at: https:// github.com/JingMog/PEARL . The released code and prompts will be distributed under a permis-sive open-source license. All external datasets and models are used only for research purposes and remain subject to their original licenses and terms of use. The released code and prompts are intended to support reproducibility and further research on LLM-based tutoring agents. 

D.6 Human Preference Annotation 

To assess whether the proposed reward model aligns with human pedagogical judgments, we con-struct a preference set containing 100 high-margin dialogue pairs. Each pair contains two tutoring di-alogues for the same problem and student setting, selected from our dialogue dataset based on score differences estimated by Gemini-3-Pro. Gemini-3-Pro is used only for pair selection, while the final preference labels are provided by human annota-tors. These pairs are not used for reward-model training or policy optimization. All the annotations were completed by two hu-man teachers with many years of teaching expe-rience. Annotators are shown the problem, refer-ence solution, student profile, and two anonymized tutor-student dialogues. They are asked to select the dialogue that provides better tutoring, consider-ing mathematical correctness, answer-leakage con-trol, progressive scaffolding, adaptivity to the stu-dent’s responses, and affective support. The order of the two dialogues is randomized, and annotators are blind to the model identities and reward-model scores. Disagreements were resolved through dis-cussion to obtain the final preference label.
